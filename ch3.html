Regression (Chapter 7)

In this section of the course, we studied regression, a statistical tool used for modeling and understanding relationships between variables. Regression models are used to establish a relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. The simplest form is linear regression, where the relationship is modeled as a straight line. However, many problems require more complex models due to the nonlinear nature of the data. Some regression techniques are:

Linear Regression: Used when the relationship between the dependent and independent variables can be described by a straight line. Least squares method is commonly used for finding the best-fit line in linear regression.

Nonlinear Regression: When the relationship between variables cannot be adequately described by a linear model, nonlinear regression is used. This involves complex functions that may include exponential, logarithmic, or trigonometric terms.

Robust Regression: This method is used to lessen the influence of outliers on the regression model, making it more resistant to anomalies in data. This is useful when the data has many outliers that we do not want to influence the regression.

Quantile Regression: Extends from estimating the mean to estimating several quantiles (for example the median or the 10th percentile), providing a more complete view of the possible outcomes of the dependent variable. It is particularly useful when we are interested in understanding variations in the distribution of the dependent variable.

Poisson Regression: Used for modeling count data and rates at which events occur in a fixed interval of time or space. This model is based on the Poisson distribution where the count outcomes have a Poisson probability distribution, and the logarithm of its expected value can be modeled as a linear combination of unknown parameters.

Regression is widely used in many contexts, but an example use case is if we must model the relationship between the mass of black holes and the velocity dispersion of stars in the galaxy bulges hosting them. Using robust regression could help mitigate the influence of atypical galaxies that do not fit the general trend, possibly due to measurement errors or unique evolutionary histories.
