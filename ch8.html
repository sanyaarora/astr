Truncated Data (Chapter 10)

In this section of the course, we discussed truncated data, or datasets that have missing values due to experimental limits. This is important for astronomy when detection limits cause incomplete datasets.

There are two different types of truncated datasets: censored data and truncated data. Censored data is where an observation falls below a detection threshold, while truncated data occurs when data points below a certain threshold are entirely absent from the dataset. Some common methods for dealing with censored or truncated data are as follows:

Survival functions: provide the probability of survival beyond a certain point of time or above a certain threshold. In astronomical studies, survival functions could help estimate the probability that a star has a brightness above a certain threshold.

Hazard functions: Describe the probability of an event occurring at a specific time, given that the event has not yet occurred. Provides a measure of the instantaneous risk of the event, conditional on survival until that time.

Kaplan-Meier Estimator: A nonparametric method for estimating the survival function from censored data. Provides a step function that gives the probability of survival past certain points.

Gehan Test: A nonparametric test that generalizes the Wilcoxon test for survival data. It compares two groups of censored data to test whether they differ significantly.

Cox Proportional-Hazards Model: Estimates hazard function without assuming a constant baseline hazard function over time, eliminating the need to specify a baseline hazard.
